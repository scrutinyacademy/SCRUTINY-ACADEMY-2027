Chapter 1: Nutrition
Autotrophic & Heterotrophic · Photosynthesis · Human Digestion · Vitamins · Malnutrition
💡
What is Nutrition? The process by which living organisms obtain food (raw materials and energy) for growth, repair and life processes. Two major types: Autotrophic (self-feeding, e.g. plants) and Heterotrophic (feed on others, e.g. animals, fungi).
🌱 AUTOTROPHIC NUTRITION — Photosynthesis

🌿 What is Photosynthesis?

Photosynthesis is the process by which plants containing the green pigment chlorophyll build up complex organic molecules (glucose) from simple inorganic ones (CO₂ + H₂O), using sunlight as energy source.

⚗️ Raw Materials Needed

  • CO₂ — absorbed through stomata
  • H₂O — absorbed by roots from soil
  • Sunlight — energy source
  • Chlorophyll — light-absorbing pigment
📊 Photosynthesis Equation (Textbook)
Sunlight 6CO₂ + 12H₂O ─────────────────▶ C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6H₂O + 6O₂ Chlorophyll (Carbon (Water) (Glucose) (Water) (Oxygen) Dioxide) Van Niel (1931): CO₂ + 2H₂O → [CH₂O] + H₂O + O₂ (simplified unit) KEY: The O₂ released comes from the SPLITTING OF WATER (not CO₂!) This was confirmed by Robert Hill — called "Hill's Reaction / Photolysis"

⚡ Two Reactions of Photosynthesis

☀️ 1. Light-Dependent Reaction (Light Reaction)

  • Occurs in GRANA (thylakoids)
  • Requires light energy
  • Chlorophyll absorbs photons → gets activated
  • Photolysis of water: H₂O → H⁺ + O₂ (O₂ released!)
  • H⁺ picked up by NADP → forms NADPH
  • ATP is also produced (energy storage)
  • Discovered by Robert Hill

🌑 2. Light-Independent Reaction (Dark Reaction)

  • Occurs in STROMA
  • Does NOT directly need light (but uses ATP + NADPH from light reaction)
  • CO₂ is fixed (combined with H from NADPH)
  • Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) is synthesized
  • Glucose → converted to starch for storage
  • Also called Calvin Cycle
⭐
Memory Trick — Light vs Dark Reaction Location Light reaction → Grana → "Light Goes to Grana" (LGG) ✓
Dark reaction → Stroma → "Dark Stays in Stroma" (DSS) ✓
NADPH and ATP are the connecting molecules between the two reactions.
📊 Flow of Photosynthesis — Step by Step
SUNLIGHT │ ▼ CHLOROPHYLL (in Grana/Thylakoids) absorbs photons │ ▼ LIGHT REACTION Photolysis of Water: 2H₂O → 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ + O₂ ↑ (released to air!) │ ├─▶ ATP formed (energy carrier) └─▶ NADPH formed (hydrogen carrier) │ ▼ DARK REACTION (Calvin Cycle) — in Stroma CO₂ + H (from NADPH) + Energy (from ATP) │ ▼ GLUCOSE (C₆H₁₂O₆) │ ▼ STARCH (storage form in plants)
🍽️ HETEROTROPHIC NUTRITION
📌
What is Heterotrophic Nutrition? Organisms that cannot make their own food and depend on other organisms (plants/animals) for nutrition. Types include: Holozoic (animals), Saprotrophic (fungi, bacteria on dead matter), Parasitic (Cuscuta, tapeworm), Insectivorous (pitcher plant).

Common Exam Confusion: Bile vs Enzyme Bile is produced by the Liver, stored in Gallbladder, secreted into duodenum. It does NOT contain any digestive enzyme — it only emulsifies (breaks into droplets) large fat globules. This increases surface area for lipase to act on.
📊 Malnutrition & Deficiency Diseases

😟 Kwashiorkor

  • Cause: Protein deficiency
  • Swollen belly (water in intercellular spaces)
  • Swollen legs, fluffy face
  • Poor muscle development, diarrhoea
  • Dry skin

😟 Marasmus

  • Cause: Deficiency of both proteins AND calories
  • Lean and very weak body
  • Less developed muscles
  • Dry, wrinkled skin, diarrhoea
  • Common in repeated/close pregnancies

⚖️ Obesity

  • Cause: Excess intake of high-calorie food
  • Excess fat accumulation
  • Risks: cardiovascular, renal, gallbladder problems
  • BMI > 30 = obese

💊 Vitamin Deficiencies

  • Vit A: Night blindness, xerophthalmia
  • Vit B1: Beri-beri
  • Vit C: Scurvy (slow wound healing)
  • Vit D: Rickets (soft bones)
  • Iron: Anaemia
  • Iodine: Goitre

Chapter 2: Respiration
Breathing · Aerobic & Anaerobic · ATP · Cellular Respiration · Lung Structure
💡
Respiration ≠ Breathing! Breathing = Physical process (moving air in & out of lungs). Respiration = Chemical process (breaking down glucose to release ATP energy inside cells). Respiration happens 24/7 in every living cell!

⚡ Cellular Respiration — ATP Production
🔋
What is ATP? ATP = Adenosine Triphosphate — the energy currency of cells. Every cellular activity (movement, growth, transport) uses ATP. When one phosphate bond is broken: ATP → ADP + Pᵢ + 7200 calories of energy released. Mitochondria = "Powerhouse of the cell" (makes most ATP).
⚖️ Aerobic vs Anaerobic Respiration

🌬️ Aerobic Respiration (with O₂)

  • Requires oxygen
  • Occurs in: Cytoplasm (glycolysis) + Mitochondria (Krebs + ETC)
  • Products: CO₂ + H₂O + 38 ATP
  • Complete oxidation of glucose
  • Example: Normal body cells

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + 38 ATP

⚡ Anaerobic Respiration (without O₂)

  • NO oxygen required
  • Occurs in: Cytoplasm only
  • Produces only 2 ATP (very little energy)
  • Two types based on organism:
  • 🦠 Yeast/Fungi: Glucose → Ethanol + CO₂ + 2 ATP
  • 💪 Muscle cells: Glucose → Lactic acid + 2 ATP
📊 Complete Respiration Pathway
GLUCOSE (C₆H₁₂O₆) │ ▼ GLYCOLYSIS (in Cytoplasm — occurs in ALL organisms) 2 Pyruvate (3-carbon) + 2 ATP │ ├─────── WITH O₂ ──────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ │ ▼ Enter MITOCHONDRIA │ Pyruvate → Acetyl CoA + CO₂ │ │ │ ▼ KREBS CYCLE (Citric Acid Cycle) — Mitochondrial Matrix │ CO₂ released, NADH + FADH₂ produced │ │ │ ▼ ELECTRON TRANSPORT CHAIN (ETC) — Inner Mitochondrial │ Membrane │ O₂ is final electron acceptor → H₂O formed │ 34 ATP produced here │ │ TOTAL = 2 (glycolysis) + 2 (Krebs) + 34 (ETC) = 38 ATP │ │ │─────── WITHOUT O₂ ─────────────────────────────────────-┘ │ ├─▶ YEAST / FUNGI: Pyruvate → Ethanol + CO₂ + 2 ATP (Fermentation) └─▶ MUSCLE CELLS: Pyruvate → Lactic Acid + 2 ATP
🦠 Fermentation

🍞 Fermentation in Yeast

Glucose → Ethanol + CO₂ + 2 ATP

Applications:
• Bread making — CO₂ makes bread rise
• Wine/Beer — ethanol produced
• Yeast = Saccharomyces cerevisiae

💪 Muscle Fatigue & Oxygen Debt

During intense exercise, O₂ supply is insufficient → muscles respire anaerobically → lactic acid accumulates → muscle cramps/pain.

After exercise, extra O₂ needed to oxidize lactic acid = "Oxygen Debt". That's why we pant after running!

🌱 Respiration in Plants
🌿
How plants exchange gases: • Stomata (leaves/green stems) — main gas exchange pores
• Lenticels (woody bark) — gas exchange in stems and roots
Plants respire 24 hours. During daytime: photosynthesis > respiration (net O₂ release). At night: only respiration (O₂ absorbed, CO₂ released).

Chapter 3: Transportation
Blood · Heart · Circulation · Xylem · Phloem · Lymph · Blood Pressure
💡
Why is Transportation needed? In large multicellular organisms, diffusion is too slow. A dedicated circulatory system rapidly transports nutrients, O₂, wastes, and hormones to every cell. Plants use xylem (water) and phloem (food) as their transport system.
❤️ Human Heart
📊 Heart Structure & Double Circulation
┌─────────────────────────────────┐ │ HEART │ From body │ RA ──valve── RV │ To lungs (deoxygenated) ──▶ (Right Atrium Right Ventricle) ──▶ Pulmonary Artery │ │ To body │ LA ──valve── LV │ From lungs (oxygenated) ◀── (Left Atrium Left Ventricle) ◀── Pulmonary Vein │ │ │ Valves: Tricuspid (right side) │ │ Bicuspid/Mitral (left) │ │ Semi-lunar (at arteries) │ └─────────────────────────────────┘ DOUBLE CIRCULATION: 1. PULMONARY: Heart ──▶ Lungs ──▶ Heart (for oxygenation) 2. SYSTEMIC: Heart ──▶ Body organs ──▶ Heart (delivers O₂) LEFT side = oxygenated blood (bright red) RIGHT side = deoxygenated blood (dark red) NEVER mix! (Septum separates them)
🩸 Blood — Components & Functions

🔴 RBC

  • Red Blood Cells
  • Contains Haemoglobin
  • Carries O₂ → Oxyhaemoglobin
  • Biconcave disc
  • NO nucleus (mature)
  • Life: ~120 days

⚪ WBC

  • White Blood Cells
  • Leucocytes
  • Fight infection/immunity
  • Produce antibodies
  • Has nucleus
  • Can squeeze through capillary walls

🟡 Platelets

  • Thrombocytes
  • Tiny cell fragments
  • Blood clotting
  • Fibrin mesh
  • Prevent excess bleeding

🌊 Plasma

  • ~55% of blood
  • Pale yellow liquid
  • Carries: glucose, amino acids, hormones, CO₂, urea
  • Contains proteins (albumin, fibrinogen)

🌿 Transportation in Plants
📊 Xylem vs Phloem — Plant Transport
XYLEM PHLOEM ───────────────────── ───────────────────── Transports WATER + minerals Transports FOOD (sucrose) Root → Stem → Leaves Leaves → All parts UNIDIRECTIONAL (upward) BIDIRECTIONAL (up & down) Dead cells (tracheids, Living cells (sieve tubes vessels, lignified) + companion cells) Strong, lignified walls Thin-walled No cytoplasm Has cytoplasm Driven by: Process called: TRANSPIRATION PULL TRANSLOCATION (evaporation from leaves creates negative pressure) WATER UPTAKE PATH: Soil → Root Hair Cell (osmosis) → Root cortex → Xylem → Stem → Leaf

💧 Transpiration

Loss of water vapour through stomata of leaves (also lenticels). Creates transpiration pull — negative pressure that pulls water upwards through xylem. Also cools the plant. Controlled by guard cells opening/closing stomata.

🔄 Lymphatic System

Lymph = tissue fluid that enters lymph vessels. Colourless, contains WBCs (lymphocytes).
Functions: drains excess tissue fluid → returns to blood | carries absorbed fats from intestine (lacteals) | immune defense (lymph nodes filter pathogens).

🩺 Blood Pressure
💉
Blood Pressure Facts BP = pressure exerted by blood on arterial walls. Measured by Sphygmomanometer.
Normal: 120/80 mmHg (120 = systolic when ventricles contract, 80 = diastolic when relaxed)
High BP (Hypertension) → risk of heart attack, stroke. Low BP (Hypotension) → dizziness, fainting.

🏆 Exam Power Tips — Chapter 3

✅

✅
EXCEPTION: Pulmonary ARTERY carries deoxygenated blood; Pulmonary VEIN carries oxygenated blood
✅
Xylem = water/minerals (upward, dead cells) | Phloem = food/sucrose (bidirectional, living cells)
✅
Haemoglobin (in RBC) + O₂ → Oxyhaemoglobin (bright red) | released to cells → Deoxyhaemoglobin (dark)
✅
Capillaries: one cell thick → allows diffusion of O₂, nutrients, CO₂ between blood and cells
✅

✅

Chapter 4: Excretion
Kidney · Nephron · Urine Formation · Dialysis · Excretion in Plants
💡
What is Excretion? Biological process of separation and removal of metabolic waste products from the body. Key waste: urea (from protein metabolism), CO₂ (from respiration), excess water and salts. Different from egestion (removal of undigested food as faeces)!
🫘 Human Excretory System
📊 Excretory System Overview
┌──────┐ ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │Kidney│ │ TWO KIDNEYS (bean-shaped) │ │ (2) │──────▶│ Located in abdomen (retroperitoneal) │ └──────┘ │ Each kidney has ~1 million nephrons │ │ │ Renal artery brings blood → filtration │ │ │ Renal vein carries clean blood away │ ▼ └──────────────────────────────────────────────┘ Ureter (2) Carries urine from kidneys to bladder │ ▼ Urinary Bladder Stores urine (capacity ~500ml) │ ▼ Urethra Carries urine out of body │ ▼ OUTSIDE BODY Urine composition: 96% water + 2.5% urea + 1.5% salts + traces of uric acid, creatinine
🔬 Nephron — The Functional Unit of Kidney
📊 Nephron Structure & Urine Formation
RENAL ARTERY │ ▼ GLOMERULUS (knot of capillaries inside Bowman's Capsule) │ ▼ STEP 1: ULTRAFILTRATION (High pressure squeezes fluid out) BOWMAN'S CAPSULE collects filtrate: → Water ✓ | Glucose ✓ | Urea ✓ | Salts ✓ ✗ Large proteins (stay in blood) ✗ RBC, WBC (too large — stay in blood) │ ▼ PROXIMAL CONVOLUTED TUBULE (PCT) STEP 2: SELECTIVE REABSORPTION → ALL glucose reabsorbed back to blood ✓ → Useful amino acids reabsorbed ✓ → Most water reabsorbed ✓ → Useful salts reabsorbed ✓ ✗ Urea stays (not reabsorbed) │ ▼ LOOP OF HENLE (U-shaped — goes into medulla) → More water reabsorbed (concentrates urine) → Salt balance adjusted │ ▼ DISTAL CONVOLUTED TUBULE (DCT) STEP 3: TUBULAR SECRETION → Extra H⁺, NH₄⁺, certain drugs secreted INTO filtrate → Fine-tunes pH balance of blood │ ▼ COLLECTING DUCT → ADH hormone controls final water reabsorption → Urine becomes concentrated │ ▼ RENAL PELVIS → URETER → BLADDER → URETHRA → OUT
⭐
Key Numbers for Exam Kidneys filter 180 litres of blood per day! But only 1.5 litres becomes urine (99% is reabsorbed!)
Urea is formed in LIVER (from excess amino acids) → transported to KIDNEYS for removal.
Normal urine: pH ~6, pale yellow (urochrome pigment), no glucose, no protein, no blood cells.
💡 Dialysis — Artificial Kidney

🏥 When is Dialysis Used?

When kidneys completely and irreversibly fail (End Stage Renal Disease — ESRD). Caused by infections, high blood pressure, diabetes, injury.

Patient's blood must be filtered artificially 3× per week, ~4 hours each session.

⚙️ How Dialysis Works

Blood is pumped out → passes through semi-permeable membrane (like nephron) in dialysate fluid → urea and toxins diffuse out (small molecules) → clean blood returned to patient.

Kidney transplant = permanent solution (living/cadaver donor).

🌱 Excretion in Plants
🌿
How do plants get rid of waste? • CO₂ — excreted through stomata and lenticels (waste of respiration)
• O₂ — excreted through stomata (waste of photosynthesis)
• Water vapour — through transpiration
• Tannins, resins, gums — stored in bark, old leaves, vacuoles; shed with leaves in autumn
• Essential oils and latex — stored in special cells (no dedicated excretory organ in plants!)
