Chapter 9: Our Environment
Ecosystem · Food Chains & Webs · Ecological Pyramids · Bioaccumulation · Biomagnification · Human Impact
🌍
What is Environment? The sum of all physical (abiotic) and biological (biotic) factors along with their chemical interactions that affect an organism. Abiotic = land, air, water, sunlight, temperature. Biotic = all living organisms (flora and fauna). The place where an organism lives = Habitat. An organism's role in its habitat = Niche.
🔗 Food Chain
📊 Food Chain — Structure & Examples
A FOOD CHAIN shows how energy passes from one organism to another. Arrows point FROM food TO feeder (direction of energy flow). GENERAL STRUCTURE: Producer → Primary Consumer → Secondary Consumer → Tertiary Consumer → Top Carnivore (Green plant) (Herbivore) (Carnivore) (Carnivore) EXAMPLES: 🌱 Grass → 🦗 Grasshopper → 🐸 Frog → 🐍 Snake → 🦅 Hawk 🌱 Grass → 🐇 Rabbit → 🦊 Fox → 🐺 Wolf 🌱 Grass → 🐐 Goat → 🧑 Man KEY FACTS: • Producers = autotrophs (green plants) — ALWAYS at base of food chain • Most food chains have 3–4 steps (limited by energy loss at each level) • At each transfer: 80–90% of energy is LOST as heat • Only 10–20% of energy passes to next trophic level • Food chains are rarely isolated — they form FOOD WEBS TROPHIC LEVELS: Level 1 = Producers (Plants) Level 2 = Primary Consumers (Herbivores — eat plants) Level 3 = Secondary Consumers (Carnivores — eat herbivores) Level 4 = Tertiary Consumers (eat secondary consumers) Level 5 = Top Carnivores (apex predators)
🕸️ Food Web
🕸️
Food Web = Interconnected Network of Food Chains In nature, organisms don't eat just one thing — they eat multiple prey and are eaten by multiple predators. This creates a complex web-like structure. Food webs are more realistic representations of energy flow in ecosystems. Removing any organism from a food web can cause a cascade effect on the entire ecosystem (e.g., Chinese Sparrow Campaign 1958).
📊 Food Web — Example (Grassland Ecosystem)
🦅 HAWK / EAGLE (Top Carnivore) / 🐍 SNAKE 🦉 OWL / \ / 🐸 FROG 🐭 RAT/MOUSE 🐦 BIRD | / | | 🦗 INSECTS 🌽 GRAIN 🐛 CATERPILLAR \ | / \____ 🌱 GREEN PLANTS ____/ (Producers) | ☀️ SUN (Primary Energy Source) NOTE: Decomposers (bacteria, fungi) break down dead matter at ALL levels and return nutrients to soil → completing the cycle
📊 Ecological Pyramids
💡
Ecological Pyramid = Graphic representation of trophic levels in shape of a pyramid. Introduced by Charles Elton (1927). Producers always at BASE. Top carnivores at TIP. Three types: Pyramid of Numbers, Pyramid of Biomass, Pyramid of Energy.

🔢 Pyramid of Numbers

Shows the count of organisms at each trophic level. Usually upright (more plants than herbivores than carnivores). Exception: Inverted when single large tree supports many insects (parasite food chain). Producers at base.

⚖️ Pyramid of Biomass

Shows the total mass (biomass) of living organisms at each level. Upright in terrestrial ecosystems. Inverted in aquatic ecosystems (phytoplankton biomass < zooplankton biomass). 10–20% biomass transferred per level.

⚡ Pyramid of Energy

Shows the total energy available at each trophic level. ALWAYS UPRIGHT (never inverted). Energy always decreases from producers to consumers. Only ~10% transferred; rest lost as heat. Most reliable pyramid.

📊 Three Types of Ecological Pyramids

⚡
10% Law / Lindemann's Law Only 10% of the energy at one trophic level is transferred to the next. The remaining 90% is lost as heat during respiration, movement, and other metabolic activities. This is why food chains rarely exceed 4–5 links — by the 4th or 5th level, barely any energy remains!
Example: 1000 kcal (grass) → 100 kcal (grasshopper) → 10 kcal (frog) → 1 kcal (snake) → 0.1 kcal (hawk)
🧪 Bioaccumulation & Biomagnification
📊 Bioaccumulation vs Biomagnification
BIOACCUMULATION = Entry and accumulation of pollutants (toxins) into a food chain/organism BIOMAGNIFICATION = Concentration of pollutants INCREASES as it moves up each trophic level HOW IT WORKS (DDT/Pesticides in Water Example): Water: DDT concentration = 0.000003 ppm (very dilute) ↓ Phytoplankton: 0.04 ppm (absorbed from water) ↓ Zooplankton: 0.5 ppm (eat phytoplankton repeatedly) ↓ Small fish: 2 ppm (eat zooplankton) ↓ Large fish: 25 ppm (eat small fish) ↓ Fish-eating bird (osprey): 75 ppm → TOXIC! (eggshell thinning, death) Human eating large fish: HIGH CONCENTRATION → Diseases WHY DOES CONCENTRATION INCREASE? • Pollutants are NOT biodegradable (cannot be broken down) • They are fat-soluble → stored in body fat, not excreted • Organisms at higher levels eat MANY organisms from lower levels • Each meal adds more toxin → concentration multiplies at each step EXAMPLES OF BIOMAGNIFICATION: • DDT in birds → Thin eggshells → Population decline • Mercury (Hg) in fish → Minamata disease in Japan (1956) • Lead, Cadmium in fish of polluted water bodies • Edulabad Water Reservoir (Medchal, Telangana) — Cd > Cr > Fe > Ni > Pb in fish tissues MINAMATA DISEASE (Japan, 1956): Caused by methyl mercury from Chisso Corporation factory (1932–1968) → Mercury accumulated in shellfish and fish → eaten by humans → severe mercury poisoning → Cats, pigs, dogs, and humans died over 36 years

✅ Biodegradable Wastes

  • Can be broken down by decomposers (bacteria, fungi)
  • Examples: Food scraps, paper, wood, cotton, leaves, animal dung, vegetable peels
  • These decompose naturally and recycle nutrients back to soil
  • Do NOT cause long-term environmental damage
  • Can be composted to make manure

❌ Non-Biodegradable Wastes

  • Cannot be broken down by decomposers — persist in environment
  • Examples: Plastics, glass, metals, synthetic fibres, DDT, polythene, nuclear waste
  • Accumulate in the environment and food chains
  • Cause biomagnification of toxins
  • Non-degradable pesticides contain mercury, arsenic, lead
🌍 Human Impact on Ecosystems

🏭 Eutrophication

Excessive nutrients (from fertilizers, sewage) enter water body → explosive growth of algae and weeds (Eichhornia, Pistia) → algae blocks sunlight → aquatic plants die → decomposers use all O₂ → fish die due to low dissolved oxygen (DO) → high Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD). Example: Kolleru Lake.

🐦 Sparrow Campaign (China, 1958)

Mass killing of sparrows to protect grain → sparrows were eating 75% insects, only 25% grain → locust population exploded (no natural predator) → locusts destroyed crops → Great Chinese Famine → millions starved. Proved: removing ANY organism from ecosystem disrupts balance.

🧪 Pesticide Problems

No "perfect pesticide" exists. Pesticides are non-selective → kill predators + pests. Non-degradable pesticides (mercury, arsenic, lead) enter food chains → Bioaccumulation → Biomagnification. Alternatives: Crop rotation, biological control, genetically resistant strains, integrated pest management.

💧 Kolleru Lake (Telangana/AP)

Largest freshwater lake in India, between W. Godavari & Krishna districts. Government declared Bird Sanctuary (1999). Pollution from: agricultural runoff, aquaculture, industrial effluents → eutrophication, low DO, high BOD, siltation → 15 local fish species endangered, migratory bird numbers dropped.

🔄 Biogeochemical Cycles — Nutrient Cycling
🔄
Cycling of Materials (Biogeochemical Cycles) Unlike energy (which flows one way and is lost as heat), materials/nutrients cycle repeatedly between organisms and environment. Dead organisms are decomposed by bacteria and fungi → minerals released back to soil and atmosphere → taken up again by plants → cycle continues. Examples: Carbon cycle, Nitrogen cycle, Water cycle, Phosphorus cycle.

Chapter 10: Natural Resources
Water · Forests · Soil · Biodiversity · Fossil Fuels · Minerals · Conservation · 4Rs
🌍
What are Natural Resources? All naturally occurring materials that humans use — air, water, soil, forests, minerals, fossil fuels, wildlife. Conservation = caring for resources so all living things can benefit now and in the future. Sustainable development = using the environment in ways that ensure resources for future generations.
♻️ Renewable vs Non-Renewable Resources

✅ Renewable Resources

  • Can be replenished/replaced after use
  • Examples: Water, Forests, Solar energy, Wind energy, Tidal energy, Biomass
  • If managed wisely, last indefinitely
  • Even water is renewable — but limitless exploitation can make it scarce!
  • UN: Water becomes scarce when annual supply < 1700 m³ per person

❌ Non-Renewable Resources

  • Cannot be replaced once used up — gone forever
  • Take millions of years to form, consumed very quickly
  • Examples: Coal, Petroleum, Natural gas, Minerals, Nuclear fuel
  • Fossil fuels formed from remains of ancient plants and animals
  • India energy: Coal 42%, Waste 24%, Oil 24%, Natural gas 7%, Nuclear 1%
💧 Water — The Most Critical Resource
📊 Water on Earth — Distribution & Availability
TOTAL WATER ON EARTH: 97% = SALT WATER (Oceans, seas, saline groundwater) — NOT USABLE directly 3% = FRESH WATER: 1.75–2% = Frozen in glaciers, ice caps, snow (~75% of freshwater) 0.7–0.8% = Groundwater and soil moisture < 0.01% = Surface water (lakes, rivers, swamps) — MOST ACCESSIBLE GROUNDWATER IN INDIA (sources of irrigation): Ground water = 43% Canals = 37% Tanks = 15% Other sources = 5% WATER SCARCITY (UN definition): Annual water supply < 1700 m³ per person = WATER SCARCE region FAO predicts: By 2025, 1.8 billion people in water-scarce regions WATER CONSERVATION METHODS: 1. Drip Irrigation — 70% reduction in water use (only 2% farmland uses it globally) 2. Sprinkler Irrigation — more efficient than flood irrigation 3. Rainwater Harvesting — soak pits, percolation tanks, contour bunding 4. Watershed Management — optimise drainage between villages 5. Dykes/Barriers — underground streams to trap groundwater 6. Crop Selection — grow low-water crops (cotton, gingelly) instead of paddy 7. Percolation Tanks — earthen/masonry dams to recharge groundwater
🏘️
Kothapally Village — Model of Sustainable Water Management ICRISAT (International Crops Research Institute for Semi-Arid Tropics) helped Kothapally village in Telangana: Built 14 water storage structures (masonry dams), 60 mini-percolation pits, 28 dry open wells recharged, promoted drip irrigation, crop rotation, community water sharing → Farmers got 250 kg more pigeon pea + 50 kg more maize per hectare. Lesson: Community-based sustainable management works!
🌳 Forests — Lungs of the Earth

🌲 Why Forests are Important

  • Forests = "lungs of the world" — absorb CO₂, release O₂
  • Habitat for 80% of world's terrestrial biodiversity
  • Regulate water cycle (transpiration, rainfall)
  • Prevent soil erosion (root systems bind soil)
  • Provide timber, medicine, food, fuel
  • Every continent except Antarctica has forests
  • Earth loses ~36 million acres of forest to deforestation per year!

🪓 Deforestation Effects

  • Destroys wildlife habitats → species extinction
  • Increases soil erosion by water and wind
  • Releases CO, CO₂, SO₂ (greenhouse gases) → global warming
  • Disrupts water cycle → irregular rainfall
  • Reduces biodiversity drastically
  • Flooding increases (no tree roots to absorb water)
📊 Sustainable Forestry & Conservation Methods
SUSTAINABLE FORESTRY PRACTICES: 1. Selective Harvesting — harvest only mature trees of specific diameter 2. Contour Strip Cropping — alternate crops across slopes to prevent erosion 3. Natural Regeneration — allow forests to regrow naturally after harvest 4. Avoid clear-cutting all high-value or large trees at once 5. Recycling paper — reduces demand for new wood (China, Mexico examples) BISHNOI MOVEMENT (Rajasthan): Amrita Devi and her daughters hugged trees to protest king's order to cut forest → Gave lives to save trees → Inspired modern Chipko Movement Bishnois: 29 rules pledging to protect nature's resources TRIBAL CONSERVATION (Telangana): Chenchu and Gond tribes — carefully extract resources without depleting them Traditional ecological knowledge (TEK) passed down generations RECOVERY (4th R): When trees cut for industry/roads → plant trees elsewhere Tree transplantation technology used to maintain forest cover
🌱 Soil Conservation
🌱
Why Soil Needs Conservation Soil is vital for food production. Poor practices: monoculture (same crop repeatedly) depletes soil nutrients. Ploughing hills → erosion. Methods of conservation: Contour strip cropping (alternating crops across slopes with different root systems), Selective harvesting (rotational cycle letting younger trees grow), Field bunding, Broad-bed furrows, Tree/grass cover to hold soil particles.
🦁 Biodiversity
🦋
Biodiversity = Variety of Living Things on Earth Essential for: food security, medicines (1000s of plant species used as medicine worldwide), ecosystem stability, clean air and water. Threats: Hunting, pollution, habitat destruction (deforestation, urbanisation). When a species becomes extinct → lost forever. Protection: National Parks, Wildlife Sanctuaries, IUCN (International Union for Conservation of Nature, founded 1948), laws against hunting.
⛽ Fossil Fuels
📊 Fossil Fuels — Formation, Types, Conservation
FORMATION: Ancient plants and animals died → buried under layers of earth → Millions of years of heat and pressure → Transformed into Coal, Petroleum (Oil), Natural Gas TYPES: Coal → Formed from ancient plant matter; used in electricity generation Petroleum → Formed from ancient marine organisms; used in vehicles, plastics Natural Gas → Methane; used for cooking (LPG), electricity generation INDIA'S ENERGY CONSUMPTION (by source): Coal = 42% (most consumed fossil fuel in India) Waste = 24% Oil = 24% Nat. Gas = 7% Nuclear = 1% Other = 2% WHY CONSERVE FOSSIL FUELS? 1. Non-renewable — once used, GONE FOREVER (no replacement) 2. Burning pollutes air → CO₂ → greenhouse effect → global warming 3. SO₂, NOx → acid rain 4. BP Review (2010): World oil reserves sufficient only for ~46.2 more years ALTERNATIVES TO FOSSIL FUELS: • Solar Energy (photovoltaic cells, solar heaters) • Wind Energy (windmills/turbines) • Hydroelectric Power (dams on rivers) • Tidal Energy (ocean tides) • Biofuels — Jatropha curcas plant seeds → biodiesel (Andhra Pradesh + Telangana: agreement with Reliance Industries; 200 acres at Kakinada) • Nuclear Energy (uranium fission — limited use due to waste issues) CONSERVATION TIPS: • Buy energy-efficient appliances • Use public transport (bus/train) instead of personal vehicles • Walk or cycle for short distances • Switch off unused lights, fans, appliances
⛏️ Minerals
⛏️
Mineral Resources — Under Threat Earth's mineral deposits being depleted rapidly. As ores for aluminium, iron become harder to find → prices rise → machinery becomes expensive. Destructive mining methods like Mountaintop Removal (MTR) destroy soil, plants, animal habitats and pollute water/air. Solutions: Less wasteful mining methods + Recycling metals (Japan: car manufacturers recycle; USA: 1/3 iron from recycled cars).
♻️ The 4Rs of Conservation
📊 4Rs — Reduce, Reuse, Recycle, Recover
1. REDUCE 🔽 Use LESS of the resource in the first place • Repair leaky taps → save water • Switch off unnecessary lights and fans → save electricity • Buy only what you need → reduce waste • Less packaging, less plastic use 2. REUSE ♻️ Use things AGAIN instead of throwing away • Reuse paper, wrapping paper, cardboard • Refill water bottles instead of buying new • Reuse old bags (cloth bags instead of plastic) • If half the world's paper were recycled → saves millions of trees 3. RECYCLE 🔄 Convert waste materials into NEW materials • Metals (aluminium, steel) — easily recyclable; saves mining • Glass → melted and reformed • Paper → pulped and remade ⚠️ Plastics — TRICKY to recycle (many types; each type only recyclable with same kind) → Need careful sorting before recycling → Some types cannot be recycled at all 4. RECOVER 🌱 When resources are used/removed → restore/recover them elsewhere • Cut trees for industry → plant trees in another area • Restore degraded land with native species • Tree transplantation technology • Revegetation of mined land CONSERVATION GROUPS: IUCN (International Union for Conservation of Nature) → Founded 1948 — alliance of governments and private groups → Monitors endangered species, threatened national parks → Proposed World Conservation Strategy (1980) → Governments use IUCN model for national conservation plans

🌡️ Key Environmental Issues

🌡️ Global Warming

Burning fossil fuels + deforestation releases CO₂, CH₄, N₂O (greenhouse gases) → traps heat in atmosphere → rising temperatures → melting glaciers → rising sea levels → climate change.

🌫️ Ozone Depletion

CFCs (from refrigerators, ACs, aerosols) → reach stratosphere → break down O₃ (ozone) → ozone hole over Antarctica → UV radiation reaches Earth → skin cancer, cataracts, reduced crop yields. Montreal Protocol (1987) phased out CFCs.

🌧️ Acid Rain

Burning coal and oil → SO₂ and NOₓ → react with water vapour → H₂SO₄ and HNO₃ (acid rain) → kills aquatic life, damages forests, erodes buildings (marble, limestone), acidifies soil.
