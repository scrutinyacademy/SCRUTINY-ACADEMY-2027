Chapter 5: Coordination
Nervous System · Neuron · Synapse · Reflex Arc · Brain · Hormones · Endocrine Glands
💡
What is Coordination? The process by which different organs and systems of the body work together to produce a proper response to stimuli. Two systems handle coordination: Nervous system (fast, electrical signals) and Endocrine system (slow, chemical signals via hormones).

⚡ Synapse — How Neurons Communicate
📊 Synapse Structure

🔄 Types of Neurons & Nerve Pathways

↑ Afferent (Sensory)

Carry impulses FROM sense organs/receptors TOWARD CNS (brain/spinal cord). Detect stimuli from environment.

↕ Association (Relay)

Connect afferent and efferent neurons. Located within CNS. Process and relay information.

↓ Efferent (Motor)

Carry impulses FROM CNS TO effector organs (muscles, glands). Produce the response/action.

⚡ Reflex Action & Reflex Arc
⚡
What is a Reflex? An automatic, involuntary, rapid response to a stimulus that does NOT involve the brain. It is processed in the spinal cord. Examples: knee-jerk reflex, pulling hand from hot object, sneezing, blinking.
📊 Reflex Arc — Complete Pathway
STIMULUS (e.g., stepping on a nail) │ ▼ RECEPTOR (pain receptors in foot — detects stimulus) │ ▼ via AFFERENT (sensory) neuron SPINAL CORD (Integration center — NOT BRAIN!) → Association neuron processes signal → Signal also sent to BRAIN (for awareness, after response) │ ▼ via EFFERENT (motor) neuron EFFECTOR (leg muscles contract — foot withdraws) │ ▼ RESPONSE (foot pulled away rapidly) PATH: Receptor → Sensory neuron → Spinal cord → Motor neuron → Effector → Response WHY SPINAL CORD (not brain)? • Speed! Bypasses brain → faster response → protects from injury • Brain only gets signal AFTER response is already happening • Example: You pull hand from hot plate BEFORE you feel the pain!

🔗 Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)
📌
PNS = all nerves outside CNS Includes cranial nerves (from brain) + spinal nerves (from spinal cord). Divided into:
Somatic NS — controls voluntary skeletal muscles (conscious control)
Autonomic NS — controls involuntary functions (heart, digestion, breathing, pupil size)
Autonomic NS has 2 divisions: Sympathetic (fight-or-flight: speeds up heart, dilates pupils) and Parasympathetic (rest-digest: slows heart, constricts pupils).

🚨 Adrenalin — Fight or Flight

  • Secreted by Adrenal gland during fear/stress/excitement
  • ↑ Heart rate & blood pressure
  • ↑ Blood glucose (energy for muscles)
  • Dilates pupil, dilates coronary artery
  • Goose bumps, hair stands erect
  • Prepares body for immediate action

🔄 Feedback Mechanism

Hormones regulate their own production. When blood glucose rises → pancreas secretes insulin → glucose decreases → insulin secretion automatically reduced. Self-correcting loop = negative feedback. Pituitary → hypothalamus loop is another example.

Chapter 6: Reproduction
Asexual & Sexual · Human Reproduction · Flowering Plants · Pregnancy · STDs
💡
What is Reproduction? Process by which living organisms produce new individuals (offspring) to continue their species. Two major types: Asexual (single parent, no gametes, genetically identical copies = clones) and Sexual (two parents, gametes, genetic variation in offspring).
🔄 Asexual Reproduction — Types

✂️ Binary Fission

  • One cell divides into two equal daughter cells
  • Examples: Amoeba, Paramecium, Bacteria
  • Can be longitudinal (Paramecium) or transverse
  • Fastest reproduction method

🫧 Budding

  • Small outgrowth (bud) develops on parent
  • Bud detaches to form new organism
  • Examples: Yeast, Hydra
  • Daughter organism genetically identical

🦎 Regeneration

  • Ability to regrow lost body parts
  • Examples: Planaria, Hydra, Starfish
  • Planaria: cut in pieces → each grows into new organism
  • Based on totipotent cells

🍄 Spore Formation

  • Formation of spores in sporangium
  • Examples: Fungi (Rhizopus), Mosses, Ferns
  • Spores are resistant, lightweight, dispersed by wind
  • Germinate in favourable conditions

🌿 Vegetative Propagation

  • New plants from vegetative parts (root, stem, leaf)
  • Natural: Runners (grass), Bulbs (onion), Rhizomes (ginger), Tubers (potato)
  • Artificial: Cutting (rose), Grafting (mango), Layering, Tissue culture
  • Advantages: preserves parent qualities, faster, seedless fruits (banana, grapes)

🔬 Fragmentation

  • Body breaks into pieces; each piece grows into new organism
  • Examples: Spirogyra (algae)
  • Different from regeneration: fragmentation is normal reproduction method

🤰 Human Pregnancy & Development
📊 Fertilization → Birth — Complete Process
Sperm meets ovum in FALLOPIAN TUBE │ ▼ FERTILIZATION (1 sperm + 1 ovum) ZYGOTE formed (diploid: 46 chromosomes) │ Mitotic divisions begin while moving down oviduct ▼ BLASTOCYST (ball of cells reaches uterus) │ ▼ IMPLANTATION (embeds in endometrium ~6-10 days after fertilization) EMBRYO develops: │ Chorion → forms PLACENTA (at ~12 weeks) │ Amnion → forms amniotic fluid (protects embryo) │ Allantois → forms UMBILICAL CORD (connects embryo to placenta) │ ▼ From 3rd month: called FOETUS PLACENTA functions: • Exchange of O₂, CO₂, nutrients, wastes (by diffusion) • NO direct blood mixing between mother and foetus • Produces pregnancy hormones (HCG, progesterone) GESTATION PERIOD = 9 months (280 days) BIRTH: uterine muscle contractions (labour pains) → baby delivered COLOSTRUM: first milk — yellowish, builds newborn immunity
🌸 Menstrual Cycle
🔄
28-Day Menstrual Cycle Day 1-5: Menstruation — Endometrium sheds (no fertilization in previous cycle)
Day 6-13: Proliferative phase — Endometrium rebuilds; Follicle matures (FSH)
Day 14: Ovulation — LH surge → Graafian follicle ruptures → Ovum released
Day 15-28: Secretory phase — Corpus luteum secretes progesterone → endometrium thickens for implantation
Menarche = first menstruation (~11-13 years) | Menopause = cessation of periods (~45-50 years)

Chapter 7: Coordination in Life Processes
Integration of Digestion · Nervous System · Hormones · Homeostasis · Sense Organs
💡
Chapter 7 Big Idea No life process works in isolation. This chapter studies how ALL body systems (digestion, circulation, excretion, nervous system, endocrine system) work TOGETHER in an integrated, coordinated manner. The key concept is homeostasis — maintaining a stable internal environment.
🍽️ How All Systems Coordinate During Digestion
📊 From Hunger to Energy — Integrated Process
STEP 1: FEELING HUNGRY Blood glucose falls → Stomach secretes GHRELIN hormone → Hunger signals sent to DIENCEPHALON (hypothalamus) in brain → Vagus nerve (10th cranial nerve) involved → Brain creates hunger sensation STEP 2: SIGHT/SMELL OF FOOD Eyes → Visual cortex (cerebrum) → signal → salivary glands Nose → Olfactory bulb → brain → conditioned reflex (Pavlov) → Saliva begins secreting BEFORE food enters mouth! STEP 3: TASTING FOOD Taste buds (papillae on tongue): Sweet, Sour, Salt, Bitter, Umami → Taste dissolved in saliva → chemoreceptors activated → Nerve signals to brain → taste identified → Smell + Taste + Temperature all combine for full flavour experience STEP 4: SWALLOWING & DIGESTION Voluntary act of swallowing → then PERISTALSIS (involuntary ANS) → Stomach: nervous system + gastrin hormone → gastric juice secretion → Small intestine: secretin + CCK hormones → bile + pancreatic juice → Villi absorb → blood carries glucose → liver processes → cells use ATP STEP 5: ENERGY UTILISATION Glucose → cellular respiration → ATP Excess glucose → liver stores as glycogen (insulin helps) Need more glucose → glucagon releases it from liver STEP 6: EXCRETION OF WASTES CO₂ → lungs Urea → kidneys Excess water/salts → kidneys + skin Bile pigments → gut → expelled as faeces
🧠 Nervous & Hormonal Coordination of Digestion

⚡ Nervous Control

  • Enteric Nervous System (ENS) — "gut brain" — 100 million neurons in gut walls
  • ANS (Vagus nerve) controls stomach acid secretion
  • Peristalsis controlled by ENS + ANS
  • Cephalic phase: sight/smell of food triggers stomach acid even before eating
  • Conditioned reflexes (Pavlov's dogs)

🧪 Hormonal Control of Digestion

  • Gastrin (stomach wall) → stimulates gastric juice & HCl
  • Secretin (duodenum) → stimulates pancreatic NaHCO₃ (neutralises acid)
  • CCK (duodenum) → stimulates bile + pancreatic enzymes release
  • GIP (duodenum) → inhibits gastric acid when fat enters
⚖️ Homeostasis — Maintaining Internal Balance
⚖️
Homeostasis = Keeping the Internal Environment Constant Examples of homeostasis in action:
• Blood glucose: Insulin ↓ / Glucagon ↑ (pancreatic feedback loop)
• Body temperature: Sweating (cooling) / Shivering (heating)
• Blood pressure: Kidney (renin-angiotensin) + heart rate adjustments
• Blood pH: Lungs (CO₂ expulsion) + Kidneys (H⁺ secretion)
• Water balance: ADH from pituitary controls kidney reabsorption

💊 Integration of Respiration, Circulation & Excretion
📊 How Body Systems Connect During Exercise
EXERCISE starts │ ├──▶ Muscles need MORE O₂ and glucose │ ├──▶ RESPIRATORY SYSTEM: breathing rate ↑ (medulla oblongata detects ↑CO₂) │ Diaphragm + intercostals contract faster → more air → more O₂ in blood │ ├──▶ CIRCULATORY SYSTEM: heart rate ↑ (sympathetic NS + adrenalin) │ Blood flow to muscles ↑ | Blood flow to digestion ↓ │ ├──▶ ENDOCRINE SYSTEM: adrenalin released → ↑heart rate, ↑blood glucose │ Glucagon released → liver converts glycogen → glucose │ ├──▶ EXCRETORY SYSTEM: sweat ↑ (thermoregulation) → kidneys concentrate urine │ CO₂ excreted via lungs ↑ | Lactic acid → liver → glucose (recovery) │ └──▶ NERVOUS SYSTEM: coordinates all of the above simultaneously! Chapter 8: Heredity & Evolution
Mendel's Laws · Genetics · DNA · Natural Selection · Darwin · Evidence of Evolution
💡
Heredity & Evolution — The Big Picture Heredity = transmission of traits from parents to offspring. Variation = differences between individuals. Evolution = gradual change in heritable traits of a population over generations. Mendel explained heredity; Darwin explained evolution through natural selection.
🫘 Mendel's Experiments
🔬
Why Mendel chose Pea Plants (Pisum sativum) Short life cycle · Many offspring · Self-pollinating (easy control) · 7 pairs of contrasting characters clearly visible · Easy to grow in large numbers · Pure breeding varieties available
📊 Mendel's 7 Contrasting Characters of Pea
CHARACTER DOMINANT (capital) RECESSIVE (small) ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────── Seed colour Yellow (Y) Green (y) Seed shape Round (R) Wrinkled (r) Stem height Tall (T) Dwarf (t) Pod colour Green (G) Yellow (g) Pod shape Inflated (I) Constricted (i) Flower colour Violet/Purple (V) White (v) Flower position Axial (A) Terminal (a)
🔬 Monohybrid Cross
📊 Monohybrid Cross — Seed Colour (Yellow × Green)
PARENTAL GENERATION (P): Pure Yellow (YY) × Pure Green (yy) GAMETES: Y, Y × y, y F1 GENERATION (First Filial): All Yy = ALL YELLOW (Y is dominant over y) → LAW OF DOMINANCE: In F1, only dominant character is expressed F1 × F1 (Self-pollination): Yy × Yy Punnett Square: Y y Y YY Yy y Yy yy F2 GENERATION (Second Filial): YY : Yy : yy = 1 : 2 : 1 (GENOTYPIC RATIO) Phenotype: YY (yellow) + Yy (yellow) + yy (green) 3 Yellow : 1 Green = 3:1 (PHENOTYPIC RATIO) KEY TERMS: • DOMINANT: expressed in F1 (Yellow = Y) • RECESSIVE: masked in F1, reappears in F2 (Green = y) • HOMOZYGOUS: same alleles (YY or yy) • HETEROZYGOUS: different alleles (Yy) • PHENOTYPE: observable trait (Yellow or Green) • GENOTYPE: genetic constitution (YY, Yy, yy) LAW OF SEGREGATION: Two alleles for a character separate during gamete formation Each gamete gets only ONE allele
🔬 Dihybrid Cross
📊 Dihybrid Cross — Seed Colour + Seed Shape
PARENT: Pure Yellow+Round (YYRR) × Pure Green+Wrinkled (yyrr) F1: All YyRr = Yellow + Round (both dominant expressed) F1 × F1: YyRr × YyRr F2 PHENOTYPIC RATIO: Yellow+Round : Yellow+Wrinkled : Green+Round : Green+Wrinkled 9 : 3 : 3 : 1 = 9 : 3 : 3 : 1 (DIHYBRID PHENOTYPIC RATIO) LAW OF INDEPENDENT ASSORTMENT: Genes for different characters are inherited independently of each other. (Colour gene and shape gene segregate independently during gamete formation)
🧬 Sex Determination in Humans
📊 Sex Determination — Chromosomal Basis
Human cells: 46 chromosomes = 44 autosomes + 2 sex chromosomes MOTHER: 44 + XX (always gives X egg) FATHER: 44 + XY (gives X sperm OR Y sperm) CROSS: Mother (XX) X X Father ───────────── X │ XX(♀) │ XX(♀)│ ← X sperm fertilizes egg │────────────────│ Y │ XY(♂) │ XY(♂)│ ← Y sperm fertilizes egg └────────────────┘ RESULT: 50% XX (Female) : 50% XY (Male) KEY POINT: SEX OF CHILD IS DETERMINED BY FATHER'S SPERM! X sperm + X egg = XX = DAUGHTER (girl) Y sperm + X egg = XY = SON (boy) Mother always contributes X only Father contributes either X or Y → determines sex
🦋 Evolution — Natural Selection

🦒 Lamarck's Theory (WRONG)

  • "Inheritance of acquired characters"
  • Giraffes stretched necks to reach leaves → passed long neck to offspring
  • Disproved by August Weismann: cut tails of rats for 22 generations → offspring always had tails
  • Bodily changes are NOT inherited (only DNA changes are)

🐦 Darwin's Theory (Natural Selection)

  • Variation exists in every population
  • Over-reproduction: more offspring than environment can support → Struggle for existence
  • Only fittest survive & reproduce = Survival of the Fittest
  • Useful variations inherited by next generation
  • Over long time → NEW SPECIES formed
  • Darwin's finches (Galapagos) → different beaks for different foods
🦴 Evidence of Evolution

🦴 Homologous Organs

Same basic structure, different functions — evidence of common ancestry
Example: Forelimb of whale (flipper), bat (wing), cheetah (running leg), mole (digging claw), human (grasping hand) — all have same humerus-radius-ulna bone arrangement, but adapted for different functions (divergent evolution)

🐦 Analogous Organs

Different structure, similar function — evidence of convergent evolution (no common ancestor)
Example: Wings of butterfly (insect) and wings of bird — both used for flight but entirely different in structure and origin.

🦷 Vestigial Organs

Reduced, non-functional organs that were useful in ancestors — evidence of evolution
Examples in humans: Coccyx (tail bone), Appendix, wisdom teeth, nictitating membrane (third eyelid), ear muscles

🪨 Fossils

Preserved remains/imprints of ancient organisms in rock layers (strata). Older fossils in deeper layers. Show gradual changes in species over time. Example: Horse evolution (Eohippus → modern horse = getting larger over millions of years)

🧬
Genetic Drift vs Natural Selection Natural Selection: Environment selects useful variations → directed change (e.g., green beetles survive because crows can't see them on leaves)
Genetic Drift: RANDOM change in gene frequency in SMALL populations due to accidents (not environment-driven). E.g., elephant stamps on beetle colony → only blue beetles survive by chance = no survival advantage, just luck.
📐 Mendel's 3 Laws Summary
LawStatementExample
Law of DominanceWhen two different alleles are present, the dominant one is expressed; recessive is masked in F1Yy → Yellow (Y dominates y)
Law of SegregationTwo alleles for a character separate during gamete formation; each gamete carries only one alleleYy parent → Y gametes and y gametes
Law of Independent AssortmentGenes for different characters assort (distribute) independently during gamete formationYyRr → YR, Yr, yR, yr gametes in equal proportion
